### 5. Candlestick Charting Explained Workbook
**File:** `Candlestick_Charting_Explained_Workbook__S_-_Gregory_Morris.pdf`
**Author:** Gregory L. Morris | **Publisher:** McGraw-Hill | **Year:** 2012 | **Pages:** 192

**KEY TOPICS:**
- Workbook/study guide companion to "Candlestick Charting Explained" (3rd ed., 2006)
- Reversal patterns (Chapter 3)
- Continuation patterns (Chapter 4)
- Pattern identification and filtering (Chapter 5)
- Integration — putting it all together (Chapter 6)
- Appendix: Other Japanese charting methods; Academic papers on candlesticks

**KEY STRATEGIES DOCUMENTED:**
- **Candle Pattern Filtering with Stochastics %D:**
  - %D above 80 = bearish presignal zone → look for bearish reversal candle patterns
  - %D below 20 = bullish presignal zone → look for bullish reversal candle patterns
  - Emotional sell signal (candle pattern) precedes technical sell signal (indicator crossover)
- Pattern filtering enhances signal quality by requiring indicator alignment

**KEY RULES EXTRACTED:**
- Single-day candlestick patterns should NOT be traded — they send a message but do not provide enough psychological evolution for reliable signals
- Candlestick patterns are best applied on **daily** charts (not intraday, not weekly)
  - Intraday: no overnight emotional development between sessions
  - Weekly: high/low can occur on different days, distorting candle meaning
- More bullish reversal patterns than bearish — because market bottoms are sharp, tops are drawn out (distribution)
- Reversal patterns are more tradable than continuation patterns
- When candle patterns occur near price-based support/resistance (horizontal lines), they perform better
- Pattern identification parameters should be strict — too loose = noise, too tight = too few signals
- Three Outside and Three Inside patterns were created by Morris/N-Squared Computing (not genuine Japanese patterns)

**RELEVANT TO:** Finn (filtering methodology is directly applicable to signal generation), Vera (understanding signal quality hierarchy)
